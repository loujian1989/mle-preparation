# Meta — Behavioral Prep

Behavioral round: 45 minutes, dedicated. Structured around Meta's 6 core values.

---

## Meta's 6 Core Values

- **Move fast**: ship early, iterate, don't wait for perfect
- **Build awesome things**: quality and craft matter; be proud of what you ship
- **Focus on long-term impact**: optimize for sustainable outcomes, not short-term metrics
- **Be bold**: take on hard problems; don't default to safe/incremental
- **Be open**: share information broadly, welcome external challenge, update positions publicly
- **Build social value**: everything connects back to connecting people / the mission

---

## What They're Actually Testing

- **Scope**: Can you operate at Meta-scale? (1B+ users, dozens of teams, massive codebases). Small-team stories don't land.
- **Influence without authority**: Meta's org is large; most Staff work requires moving people you don't manage.
- **Data-driven decisions**: Did you define success metrics before or after? Did you change course based on evidence?
- **Speed with quality**: Can you move fast AND reliably? Meta wants both; any answer that trades one for the other needs justification.
- **Openness**: Do you give and receive critical feedback directly? Do you publicly update your view when wrong?

| "Tell me about growing your team's capabilities." | Build awesome things | `technical-leadership.md` — Template 3 |

## Signals That Differentiate Staff from Senior

- **Scope**: Senior = team-level. Staff = org-level. If your story affects 1 team, it's not Staff at Meta.
- **Quantification**: Meta expects specific numbers. "Improved by 15%" beats "improved significantly."
- **The hard part**: interviewers will probe "what was the hardest part?" — if the hard part was technical, you're likely telling a Senior story. If the hard part was organizational/interpersonal, that's Staff.
- **"We" vs. "I"**: Meta interviewers are trained to notice "we" and ask "what was YOUR specific contribution?" Be ready to answer this for every action.

---

## Red Flags (Lower the Bar)

- Stories that are exclusively technical — no cross-functional component
- "It went well" endings without specific metrics
- Waiting for direction from manager rather than taking ownership
- "I raised the concern" without driving resolution
- Asking permission for everything — Meta values judgment and autonomy
